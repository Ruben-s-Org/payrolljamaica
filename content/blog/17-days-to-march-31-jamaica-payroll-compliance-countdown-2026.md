## The Rates That Must Be Right

Every deduction in your payroll should have been calculated using these exact rates for the 2025/26 tax year. If even one rate was wrong for even one month, your annual totals will be off:

| Deduction | Employee Rate | Employer Rate | What to Watch For |
|---|---|---|---|
| **PAYE** | Progressive | N/A | Threshold is **J$1,500,096/year** (J$125,008/month) |
| **NIS** | 3% | 3% | Subject to the NIS wage ceiling |
| **NHT** | 2% | 3% | Applies to gross emoluments — no ceiling |
| **Education Tax** | 2.25% | 3.5% | Applied after NIS and NHT deductions |
| **HEART/NSTA** | — | 3% | Employer-only; often missed by small businesses |

**The PAYE threshold is the one that trips people up most.** J$1,500,096 per year means the first J$125,008 per month is tax-free. If you have employees earning close to that threshold — say J$130,000 or J$140,000 monthly — a small error in how the threshold was applied could mean you under-deducted or over-deducted PAYE every single month. Multiply that by 12 months, and the discrepancy on your SO1 will be noticeable.

## NHT: The Compliance Area Employers Overlook

Most employers pay close attention to PAYE and NIS. NHT? That's the one that slips through the cracks.

The National Housing Trust contribution is straightforward — 2% from the employee, 3% from the employer, applied to total gross emoluments with no ceiling. But "straightforward" doesn't mean employers always get it right. Here's where NHT issues typically surface:

**Missing NHT registration numbers.** Every employee needs an NHT number. If you hired someone mid-year and never got them registered, their contributions have been going into a holding pattern. NHT will eventually match them, but it creates noise in your annual return and can delay processing. Get any unregistered employees sorted at [nht.gov.jm](https://nht.gov.jm) this week.

**Incorrect calculation base.** NHT is calculated on gross emoluments — that includes allowances, bonuses, and overtime. Some employers calculate NHT only on basic salary, which understates the contribution. If you paid a Christmas bonus in December and didn't include it in that month's NHT calculation, your total is short.

**The employer share is higher than the employee share.** Unlike NIS where both sides pay 3%, the employer pays 3% NHT while the employee pays 2%. This 5% total is sometimes miscalculated as 4% (2% + 2%) by employers who assume the rates are equal.

Run the numbers. The [PayrollJamaica calculator](https://payrolljamaica.com/calculator) breaks out NHT for both the employee and employer share so you can verify the split.

## How to Verify Your Year-End Figures: A Practical Approach

You have 12 months of payroll data. Checking every line for every employee sounds overwhelming. Here's how to make it manageable:

### Step 1: Spot-Check Three Employees

Pick three employees with different pay profiles:
- One who earned the same amount every month (your simplest case)
- One who had a salary change mid-year
- One who received bonuses or overtime

For each, calculate their full-year deductions using the [PayrollJamaica calculator](https://payrolljamaica.com/calculator) and compare against your payroll records. If all three match, your system is likely calculating correctly across the board. If even one is off, you need to check everyone.

### Step 2: Total Your S02 Remittances

Add up the 12 monthly S02 amounts you remitted to TAJ. This total must equal the sum of all employee deductions plus employer contributions for the full year. If these don't balance, there's either a calculation error or a missed/double payment somewhere in the year.

### Step 3: Cross-Reference NHT and NIS Separately

Don't just check the lump totals. Break out NHT contributions and NIS contributions separately. It's common for PAYE to be correct while NHT or NIS is slightly off — especially after mid-year hires or salary adjustments.

## Real Talk: What Happens If Your Numbers Are Wrong

Yuh might be thinking, "Mi just file wha mi have and sort it out later." Understandable. But here's what "sort it out later" actually looks like:

1. **TAJ sends an assessment** — they compare your SO1 against their S02 records and if the numbers don't match, they issue an assessment for the difference plus interest
2. **You owe the difference plus penalty** — even if you overpaid in some months and underpaid in others, TAJ calculates based on each month's shortfall, not the net
3. **NHT follows up separately** — NHT conducts its own reconciliation and if your annual return doesn't match their records, you receive a separate demand
4. **NIS discrepancies affect employees** — incorrect NIS records can affect an employee's benefit entitlements down the road, which becomes your problem when they come asking questions

Getting it right now takes an afternoon. Getting it wrong takes months to unravel. Choose the afternoon.
