# How to Do Payroll in Jamaica: Step-by-Step Guide for Employers 2026

If you've just hired your first employee — or if you've been running payroll manually and suspect you're getting something wrong — this guide is for you.

Payroll in Jamaica involves more moving parts than most employers expect. You're not just calculating a salary and writing a cheque. You're withholding PAYE, contributing to the NHT, calculating NIS, deducting Education Tax, generating compliant payslips, and filing monthly S01 returns to the Tax Administration Jamaica. Get any one of these wrong and you face penalties, back payments, and potentially a TAJ audit.

This guide walks through every step, in order, with the correct 2026 rates.

---

## How to Calculate NIS in Jamaica

NIS (National Insurance Scheme) contributions in Jamaica are calculated at 3% of gross pay for employees and 3% for employers, applied to earnings up to the maximum insurable earnings ceiling. For 2026, the maximum insurable wage is J$416,667 per month (J$5,000,000 per year) — once an employee's cumulative earnings reach this cap, NIS contributions stop for the remainder of the tax year.

## Before You Run Your First Payroll: Registration

Before you can legally run payroll in Jamaica, you need to be registered with the relevant statutory agencies.

### 1. Register with the Tax Administration Jamaica (TAJ)
You need a Tax Registration Number (TRN) as an employer. If you have one as an individual, you'll need a separate one for the business. Register at any TAJ Revenue Service Centre or online at [TAJ e-Services](https://www.jamaicatax.gov.jm).

Once registered, you'll receive your Employer TRN and become responsible for monthly PAYE remittances and annual P24 filings.

### 2. Register with the National Housing Trust (NHT)
Employer registration with the NHT is mandatory if you employ anyone. You'll need your Employer TRN and basic business details. NHT contributions are due monthly.

### 3. Register with the National Insurance Scheme (NIS)
Register each employee individually with the NIS. Each employee must have an NIS number — if they don't have one, you must assist them in getting one before you can process their first payroll. NIS contributions are compulsory.

### 4. Collect Employee Information
For each employee you'll need:
- Full legal name (as on national ID)
- TRN (Tax Registration Number)
- NHT number
- NIS number
- Date of birth (for pension/age-related tax adjustments)
- Banking details for direct deposit
- Signed employment contract specifying salary, pay period, and benefits

## Step 1: Determine Gross Pay

Gross pay is the total compensation before any deductions. This includes:
- **Basic salary** — the agreed fixed wage
- **Overtime** — in Jamaica, overtime for work beyond 40 hours/week is typically paid at 1.5× the regular hourly rate (verify per employment contract and the Employment (Equal Pay for Men and Women) Act)
- **Allowances** — housing, transport, meal allowances (note: some allowances are taxable; check TAJ guidance)
- **Commissions and bonuses** — fully taxable as income
- **Service charge** (hospitality sector) — 10% added to hotel and restaurant bills, allocated to employees; subject to PAYE

For a **monthly-paid employee** earning J$120,000/month basic, gross pay before overtime or bonuses would be J$120,000.

## Step 2: Calculate PAYE (Pay As You Earn)

PAYE is the income tax withheld from employee salaries and remitted to the TAJ on the employee's behalf.

### 2026 PAYE Rates and Thresholds

| Income Bracket | Rate |
|---|---|
| Below J$125,008/month (J$1,500,096/year) | 0% — exempt |
| J$125,009 – J$500,000/month (up to J$6,000,000/year) | 25% |
| Above J$500,000/month (above J$6,000,000/year) | 30% |

**Note:** Employees aged 65+ have a higher tax-free threshold. Always verify the employee's date of birth.

### How to Calculate PAYE — Example

Employee gross monthly salary: **J$180,000**

1. Monthly tax-free threshold: **J$125,008**
2. Taxable income: J$180,000 − J$125,008 = **J$54,992**
3. PAYE at 25%: J$54,992 × 0.25 = **J$13,748**

Monthly PAYE to withhold and remit: **J$13,748**

**Important:** PAYE is calculated on a **cumulative basis** across the tax year (April 1 to March 31). If an employee's income varies month to month, you'll need to track year-to-date gross income and year-to-date PAYE withheld to ensure the annual liability is met correctly. Most payroll software handles this automatically.

## Step 3: Calculate NHT (National Housing Trust) Contributions

| Contribution | Rate | Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Employee NHT | 2% | Gross wages |
| Employer NHT | 3% | Gross wages |

For the J$180,000 gross salary example:
- **Employee NHT deduction:** J$180,000 × 2% = **J$3,600**
- **Employer NHT contribution:** J$180,000 × 3% = **J$5,400** (this is a cost to the employer, not deducted from the employee)

**NHT is tied to individual NHT numbers.** Contributions must be reported against each employee's NHT number so they accumulate toward housing benefits. Mis-reporting or omitting NHT numbers results in employees losing their contribution history.

**Late payment penalty:** 20% per annum on outstanding NHT contributions. This is steep — do not miss NHT deadlines.

## Step 4: Calculate NIS (National Insurance Scheme) Contributions

| Contribution | Rate | Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Employee NIS | 3% | Insurable wages, capped at J$416,667/month |
| Employer NIS | 3% | Same cap |

The **maximum insurable wage** is J$5,000,000/year (J$416,667/month). If your employee earns more than J$416,667/month, NIS is calculated on J$416,667, not the full gross.

For the J$180,000 gross salary example:
- **Insurable wage:** J$180,000 (below the J$416,667/month cap)
- **Employee NIS:** J$180,000 × 3% = **J$5,400**
- **Employer NIS:** J$180,000 × 3% = **J$5,400** (employer cost)

**Track each employee's cumulative NIS insurable wages.** Once they've reached J$5,000,000 for the year, NIS contributions stop for the remainder of the tax year and resume April 1.

---

## Step 5: Calculate Education Tax

Education Tax is often the most frequently missed deduction because it doesn't have its own separate registration or return — it's filed on the PAYE return alongside income tax. Many employers incorrectly omit it.

| Contribution | Rate | Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Employee Education Tax | 2.25% | Gross wages |
| Employer Education Tax | 3.5% | Gross wages |

For the J$180,000 gross example:
- **Employee Education Tax:** J$180,000 × 2.25% = **J$4,050**
- **Employer Education Tax:** J$180,000 × 3.5% = **J$6,300** (employer cost)

---

## Step 6: Calculate Net Pay

Net pay is what the employee actually receives:

**Gross Pay: J$180,000**

| Deduction | Amount |
|---|---|
| PAYE | J$12,398 |
| NHT (employee) | J$3,600 |
| NIS (employee) | J$5,400 |
| Education Tax (employee) | J$4,050 |
| **Total Deductions** | **J$25,448** |

**Net Pay: J$154,552**

Note: The employer also bears their own NHT (J$5,400), NIS (J$5,400), and Education Tax (J$6,300) contributions — totalling J$17,100 in employer payroll costs on top of the gross salary. The **true cost of this employee to the employer is J$197,100/month**, not J$180,000.

## Step 7: Generate Compliant Payslips

Every employee must receive a payslip for each pay period. Jamaican payslips should include, at minimum:
- Employee name and TRN
- Pay period dates
- Gross pay breakdown (basic, overtime, allowances)
- Each deduction itemised: PAYE, NHT, NIS, Education Tax, any voluntary deductions (pension, union dues)
- Net pay
- Year-to-date gross and year-to-date PAYE withheld

Payslips can be paper or digital. Employees have the right to receive them.

---

## Step 8: File Monthly S01 Returns and Remit Payments

By the **14th of each month**, you must:

1. **File the S01 return** with the TAJ — this summarises your total payroll, each employee's PAYE withheld, Education Tax, NHT contributions, and NIS contributions for the prior month.
2. **Remit all amounts** — PAYE, Education Tax, NHT, and NIS are all due by the 14th.

**Important:** NHT and NIS have separate remittance processes even though they appear on the same S01 return. Confirm with each agency's payment portal that funds have been applied correctly.

**Penalties for late filing:**
- TAJ: Interest at the rate of 16.62% per annum + penalties
- NHT: 20% per annum penalty
- NIS: penalties apply

---

## Step 9: Annual Filings and Year-End

At the close of the tax year (March 31), you have additional obligations:

| Filing | Deadline | Description |
|---|---|---|
| S01 Annual Return | March 31 | Summary of all payroll and deductions for the year |
| NHT Annual Return | March 31 | Summary of all NHT contributions per employee |
| P24 (Employer Annual Return) | April 30 | Total remuneration and PAYE per employee |
| P45 (Employee Departure) | Within 30 days of departure | Issued to employees who leave during the year |

The **March 31 deadline is critical** — late S01 and NHT Annual Returns attract significant penalties.

## Common Payroll Mistakes Jamaican Employers Make

**1. Using the wrong tax threshold**
Jamaica's PAYE threshold changes periodically. Using an outdated threshold causes you to over- or under-withhold PAYE — both create problems. The 2026 annual threshold is J$1,500,096.

**2. Missing the NIS wage cap**
Continuing to deduct and remit NIS contributions after an employee has hit J$5,000,000 for the year is an overpayment that's complex to correct. Track cumulative wages per employee.

**3. Omitting Education Tax**
Because Education Tax doesn't have a separate form, many employers simply forget it. It's included on the S01 — omitting it creates a discrepancy that the TAJ will flag.

**4. Not tying NHT contributions to NHT numbers**
NHT contributions must be linked to individual NHT numbers. A lump-sum payment without employee-level detail is rejected by the NHT or applied incorrectly, meaning employees don't get credit for contributions.

**5. Running payroll on gross pay before benefits-in-kind**
Some benefits — company vehicles used for personal travel, housing provided by the employer above certain thresholds — are taxable benefits-in-kind and should be included in gross income for PAYE purposes. Check with your tax advisor.
