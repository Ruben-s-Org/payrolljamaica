## What Drives Payroll Software Pricing in Jamaica

Payroll software is almost always priced per employee, per month. The logic is simple: more employees means more payroll runs, more tax filings, more complexity. Vendors charge accordingly.

In the Jamaican market, pricing typically depends on:

- **Number of active employees** on payroll
- **Pay frequency** — weekly, bi-weekly, or monthly
- **Compliance modules** — automatic PAYE updates, NIS, NHT, Education Tax
- **Reporting features** — TD4 generation, statutory remittance summaries
- **Support tier** — self-serve vs. dedicated account manager

Some platforms add fees for year-end TD4 filing, additional users, or API integrations. Read the fine print.

## The Real Cost of NOT Using Payroll Software

Before you decide software is too expensive, calculate the cost of your current approach.

### If You're Using Excel

A business owner or office manager doing payroll manually in Jamaica typically spends:

- **3–6 hours per payroll run** for a 10-person team
- **Additional 4–8 hours at year-end** preparing TD4 forms
- **Unpaid time reacting to tax table changes** — TAJ updates PAYE thresholds, NIS rates shift, Education Tax adjustments happen

At a conservative value of J$2,500/hour for your time (or your HR person's time), a monthly payroll for 10 employees costs you:

**J$2,500 × 5 hours = J$12,500/month in labor cost alone**

That's before you factor in errors.

### Cost of Payroll Errors in Jamaica

A single PAYE miscalculation can result in:

- **Penalties from TAJ** — interest accrues at 40% per annum on underpayments
- **Employee complaints** — wrong NHT deductions affect housing benefit calculations
- **Year-end corrections** — reissuing TD4s costs time and credibility

One error on a payroll for a J$150,000/month employee can easily cost you J$30,000–J$60,000 in penalties, corrections, and professional fees to fix it. Payroll software that costs J$8,000/month starts looking very cheap in that light.

## What You Should Expect in Any Jamaican Payroll Software

Regardless of price tier, any payroll software sold in Jamaica in 2026 should include:

### Automatic Tax Compliance

Jamaica's tax rates change. The current PAYE income tax threshold is **J$1,500,096 per year** (J$125,008/month). NIS employee contribution is **3%** of insurable earnings (capped). NHT is **2% employee / 3% employer**. Education Tax is **2.25% employee**.

These rates are not static. When TAJ updates them, your software should update automatically — not require you to manually edit formulas in a spreadsheet.

### TD4 Generation

Your software should generate compliant TD4 forms for every employee at year-end. This is not optional — it's a legal requirement. If you're paying for software that makes you generate TD4s manually or export data to another tool, you're overpaying for an incomplete product.

### Statutory Remittance Reports

Monthly NIS, NHT, and Education Tax remittances require specific breakdowns. Good software generates these reports ready to submit, so your accountant or bookkeeper doesn't have to rebuild them from scratch.

### Audit Trail

Every payroll run should be logged with timestamps and approval records. This protects you in a TAJ audit.

## Cheap Payroll Software Jamaica: What to Watch Out For

"Cheap" doesn't always mean bad value — but it can mean limited features that cost you more in the long run.

Watch out for:

**Hidden fees.** Some low-cost platforms charge extra for:
- Generating TD4s (sometimes J$500–J$1,000 per employee)
- Running payroll more than once per month
- Adding more than one admin user
- Accessing historical data after a certain period

**No local compliance updates.** Generic international payroll software doesn't know about Jamaica's specific PAYE thresholds or NHT rates. You end up maintaining tax tables yourself — which defeats the purpose.

**Poor support.** When your payroll is wrong and payday is tomorrow, you need someone who understands Jamaican payroll, not a generic support queue in another timezone.

**No audit history.** If TAJ audits your business, you need to show payroll records going back years. Make sure your software retains this data and lets you export it.

## How to Choose the Right Tier

**1–5 employees:** Start with a basic plan. You don't need advanced HR features yet. Prioritize accurate PAYE and NIS calculations, and TD4 generation at year-end.

**6–20 employees:** Move to a standard plan. You'll benefit from multi-user access (so your bookkeeper and you can both work in the system) and more detailed reporting.

**21–50 employees:** Look for plans that include leave management integration, departmental reporting, and strong support SLAs. Payroll errors at this scale are expensive.

**50+ employees:** You likely need enterprise features — multi-location support, role-based permissions, integration with accounting software (QuickBooks, Sage), and dedicated support.
