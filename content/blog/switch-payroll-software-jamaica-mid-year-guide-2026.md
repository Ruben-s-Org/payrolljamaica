# How to Switch Payroll Software in Jamaica Mid-Year (Without Losing Your Data)

Many Jamaican businesses consider switching payroll software after a bad March 31 experience — missed deadlines, wrong PAYE calculations, or hours spent manually preparing the SO1 return. But they hesitate because switching mid-year feels risky.

The good news: switching payroll software mid-year in Jamaica is completely manageable if you follow the right process. This guide walks you through every step.

---

## When Is the Best Time to Switch Payroll Software in Jamaica?

The ideal time to switch is at the **start of a new tax year (April 1)**. All year-to-date accumulators reset to zero, making data entry in the new system straightforward. You also get a full year to build up clean records before the next SO1 deadline.

However, mid-year switching (any month from April to February) is also viable if:
- Your current software is giving you wrong PAYE/NIS/NHT calculations
- You are spending more than 2 hours per payroll run
- You keep getting TAJ penalty notices
- Your accountant is flagging errors in the SO1 data

**Avoid switching in February or March** — these are the highest-risk months because the SO1 and PAYE reconciliation deadline (March 31) is imminent.

## What Data You Need Before You Switch

Before migrating to a new payroll system, collect the following from your existing system:

### Employee Master Data
- Full name, TRN, NIS number, NHT number
- Date of birth, address, contact details
- Employment start date, position, department
- Pay frequency (monthly, fortnightly, weekly)
- Salary or hourly rate
- Pay type (salary, hourly, commission, mixture)

### Year-to-Date (YTD) Figures for Each Employee
This is the most critical data for a mid-year switch. You need:
- YTD gross earnings
- YTD PAYE deducted
- YTD NIS contributions (employee and employer)
- YTD NHT contributions (employee and employer)
- YTD Education Tax (employee and employer)
- YTD net pay

**Why YTD matters:** Your new system needs these totals to correctly calculate statutory deductions for the remainder of the tax year. Without them, calculations will be wrong and the SO1 will not balance.

### Special Considerations
- Employees approaching the NIS annual ceiling (JMD $5,000,000 gross) — check if any should already have stopped paying NIS
- Employees on maternity leave, sick leave, or suspension
- Directors with different PAYE treatment (director's fees vs salary)

### Step 3: Enter Opening Balances (Critical)

Opening balances ensure PayrollJamaica knows exactly how much PAYE, NIS, NHT, and Education Tax has already been deducted this year.

For each employee, enter:
- YTD gross earnings (before any deductions)
- YTD PAYE deducted
- YTD employee NIS

- YTD employee NHT

- YTD employee Education Tax
- YTD employer Education Tax

**Verification check:** After entering opening balances, run a YTD report in PayrollJamaica and compare it to your old system. The totals should match exactly.

### Step 4: Run a Parallel Payroll (Recommended)

For your first payroll run in the new system, run the same payroll in both old and new systems and compare the output:
- Are gross pay amounts identical?
- Do PAYE deductions match?
- Do NIS/NHT/Education Tax deductions match?
- Is net pay the same?

Any discrepancy must be investigated before going live. Common causes:
- Missing YTD data for an employee
- Wrong pay frequency (monthly vs fortnightly threshold calculation)
- Incorrect allowance setup (some allowances are PAYE-taxable, some are not)

## Mid-Year Switch: PAYE Reconciliation

Switching mid-year means your SO1 return at March 31 will have data from two systems. To prepare a clean SO1:

1. Export the full-year PAYE summary from **both systems**
2. Add the YTD totals from the old system to the YTD totals from the new system
3. The combined total is what you declare on the SO1

PayrollJamaica's opening balance system handles this automatically — when you run the March 31 SO1 report, it includes the opening balances you entered at migration.

## Switching From Excel to PayrollJamaica

The most common migration in Jamaica is from Excel-based payroll. This carries an additional risk: Excel calculations are often inconsistent — different formulae for different months, manual overrides that were never corrected.

Before entering Excel YTD figures into PayrollJamaica, **verify the Excel totals manually** for at least 3 employees:
1. Add up each month's PAYE column → should match the YTD PAYE figure
2. Recalculate the most recent month's PAYE using the [PAYE calculator](/paye-calculator-jamaica)
3. If the manual calculation differs from Excel, investigate which is correct

Do not carry over Excel errors into your new system. This is a fresh start.

## Frequently Asked Questions About Switching Payroll Software in Jamaica

**Q: Can I switch payroll software during the year without affecting my March 31 SO1?**
Yes. As long as you enter accurate opening balances (YTD figures from your old system), PayrollJamaica will correctly calculate total annual PAYE for each employee, and the SO1 will balance.

**Q: What happens to payslips from before the switch?**
Your previous payslips remain with your old system. You do not need to re-generate them. PayrollJamaica generates all payslips from your go-live date forward.

**Q: How long does a migration take?**
For a business with under 20 employees, migration typically takes 2–4 hours (setup + data entry + parallel run verification). For 20–100 employees, allow 1–2 days. PayrollJamaica support assists with migrations at no extra cost.

**Q: Do I need my accountant to help with the migration?**
Not necessarily. However, it is good practice to have your accountant verify the opening balances match your trial balance payroll accounts.

**Q: What if I discover my old system was calculating PAYE incorrectly?**
This is more common than you might expect. You have two options:
1. Correct the opening balances to match what *should* have been deducted (correct approach)
2. Use the old system's (incorrect) figures to maintain consistency for the SO1 (practical approach if the discrepancy is small)
For significant discrepancies, consult a tax practitioner about whether to file an amended PAYE return with TAJ.
