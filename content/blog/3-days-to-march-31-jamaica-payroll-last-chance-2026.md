# 3 Days to March 31: Don't Forget the NHT Annual Return (It's Separate from SO1)

Three days left. You may have already filed your SO1 Annual Return with Tax Administration Jamaica. You may have a confirmation receipt sitting in your email. You may feel, quite reasonably, that your March 31 obligations are handled.

Here is the question: have you also filed your NHT Annual Return?

Not your SO1. Not your monthly NHT remittances. The NHT Annual Return — a separate document, filed through a separate portal, to a separate government agency, with a separate penalty structure.

If you are not certain you have filed it, this post is for you. Because every year, a significant number of Jamaican employers who genuinely believe they are compliant receive a penalty notice from the National Housing Trust in June or July. Not because they forgot to pay their NHT contributions. They made those payments faithfully every month. But because they did not file the annual return that reconciles those payments — and the NHT, unlike TAJ, does not always send loud reminders.

---

## What the NHT Annual Return Is and Why It Exists

The National Housing Trust is funded by mandatory contributions from every employed Jamaican and their employer. Employees contribute 2% of gross wages. Employers contribute 3% of the gross wages of all their employees. These contributions are supposed to be remitted monthly, typically alongside PAYE.

But the monthly remittances alone are not enough. The NHT requires an annual reconciliation that confirms:

- Every employee who worked for you during the calendar year is accounted for
- The total contributions declared match the total contributions received
- Each employee's individual contribution history is correctly recorded against their NHT beneficiary number

This annual reconciliation is the NHT Annual Return. Without it, the NHT cannot properly credit each employee's individual NHT account — which affects their eligibility to access NHT benefits, including housing loans. And without it, the NHT cannot confirm that you, as an employer, have met your annual compliance obligation.

The NHT Annual Return is due by **March 31** — the same deadline as the SO1. But it is filed through the NHT employer portal, not through eTAJ. Many employers treat them as one task when they are two completely separate filings.

---

## What Happens If You Don't File the NHT Annual Return

The NHT penalty for late or missing annual returns is a **20% surcharge** on outstanding contributions. Unlike TAJ, which applies a tiered penalty structure, the NHT applies its 20% surcharge immediately when a return is identified as missing or late, and interest accrues on the outstanding balance from that point.

More importantly for employees: every year of NHT Annual Return that is not filed correctly is a year where your employees' NHT benefit eligibility is in question. To be eligible for an NHT housing loan, a beneficiary needs a continuous record of contributions. A gap in their contribution history — caused by your failure to file the annual return that would have reconciled and recorded those contributions — can disqualify them from a loan or cause significant delays in their application.

For employees who have been saving through NHT contributions for years toward a housing loan, this is not a minor inconvenience. It is a direct impact on one of the most significant financial events of their life.

As an employer, you are legally required to file the return. The 20% surcharge is the financial consequence. The harm to your employees is the human one.

## How the NHT Annual Return Differs from the SO1

These two returns cover overlapping data but serve different purposes and go to different agencies.

| | SO1 Annual Return | NHT Annual Return |
|---|---|---|
| Filed with | Tax Administration Jamaica (TAJ) | National Housing Trust (NHT) |
| Portal | etax.gov.jm | NHT employer portal |
| Primary purpose | PAYE reconciliation | NHT contribution reconciliation |
| Employee data required | TRN, income, PAYE deducted | TRN, NHT beneficiary number, contributions |
| Employer data required | PAYE file number | NHT employer number |
| Late penalty | 10% + 20% surcharge + 2% monthly interest | 20% surcharge + interest |

The NHT Annual Return requires some data that the SO1 does not:

**NHT Beneficiary Numbers:** Each employee has an NHT beneficiary number that is separate from their TRN and their NIS number. This is the number tied to their NHT account. You need it for every employee on the NHT Annual Return.

If you do not have NHT beneficiary numbers for all your employees, collect them today. Employees can find their NHT beneficiary number on their NHT contribution statement (available by calling the NHT or logging into the NHT online portal), on their NHT correspondence, or sometimes on payslips if your payroll system captures it.

**Monthly contribution breakdowns per employee:** The NHT Annual Return requires the monthly contribution for each employee, not just an annual total. This means you need your monthly NHT payment receipts and the allocation of each payment across employees.

## Step-by-Step: Filing the NHT Annual Return

### Step 1: Access the NHT Employer Portal

Go to the NHT's online employer services portal. You will need your NHT employer registration number and your registered portal credentials. If you have never logged in online, you can register on the NHT website using your employer number, or file in person at any NHT office.

### Step 2: Verify Your Employer Details

Confirm your registered address, contact information, and employer NHT number are current. If your business address or contact has changed since you last updated the NHT, update it before filing the return.

### Step 3: Prepare Your Employee Contribution Data

For each employee who worked for you at any point during 2025, you will need:
- Full legal name
- TRN
- NHT beneficiary number
- Monthly contribution amounts (employee 2% and employer 3%) for each month employed
- Start and end dates for employees who did not work the full year

The employee contribution (2%) plus employer contribution (3%) should equal 5% of each employee's gross wages per period. If your records show different numbers, identify the source of the discrepancy before filing.

### Step 4: Enter or Upload Contribution Data

Depending on the number of employees, you can enter data manually or upload a file. For businesses with more than 5 employees, the upload function is significantly faster. The NHT accepts contributions data in a standard format — download the template from the employer portal.

### Step 5: Reconcile with Your Payment History

The NHT will compare your declared contributions against contributions received on your account. If you made all monthly NHT payments correctly and they were allocated to your employer account, this reconciliation should clear. If there are gaps — payments not received, payments allocated incorrectly, or months where NHT was remitted with PAYE but not separately tracked — address them before submitting.

### Step 6: Submit and Save Confirmation

Submit the return and save your confirmation reference number. The NHT confirmation is separate from your eTAJ confirmation. You need both.

## The Common Scenario: You Paid but Never Filed

The most common NHT compliance problem for Jamaican employers is not non-payment — it is payment without the corresponding annual reconciliation. Employers who make their monthly NHT contributions via salary deduction, remit them through the bank, and never log into the NHT portal at year-end fall into this category.

The monthly payment satisfies your payment obligation. It does not satisfy your filing obligation. The annual return is required regardless of whether you made all your monthly payments correctly.

Think of it this way: the monthly payments are the money. The annual return is the accounting. TAJ and NHT need both. Filing the annual return without paying leaves you owing money. Paying without filing the annual return leaves you with an unreconciled account — which NHT treats as a compliance failure with its own penalty.

If you made all your monthly NHT contributions in 2025 but never filed the NHT Annual Return, filing it now confirms that the payments made were correct and allocated properly. There will be no penalty if the contributions were fully paid and the return is filed by March 31.

## If You Have NHT Contribution Arrears

Some employers will be reading this and realising they not only have not filed the NHT Annual Return — they have also not made all their monthly contributions. Missed monthly NHT payments are a more serious issue than missing only the annual return.

Here is the situation you are in: every month of missed NHT contributions represents:
- Employee 2% that was deducted from payroll but not remitted (this is money that belongs to your employees and is in your account)
- Employer 3% that was never paid
- 20% NHT surcharge on the combined outstanding amount when it is identified
- Interest accruing from each missed contribution period

If you are in this situation, you need to:

1. Calculate the total outstanding NHT contributions for every missed month
2. Remit the outstanding amounts as soon as possible — before March 31 if at all possible, since any payment before the deadline reduces the outstanding balance that the 20% surcharge is applied to
3. File the NHT Annual Return by March 31, accurately reflecting what was paid and what is outstanding

The NHT has an employer compliance helpline — call them. They deal with employers in arrears on a regular basis. The 20% surcharge is automatic once the return is assessed, but payment arrangements can be worked out for the principal.

## The Third Return: NIS Reconciliation

While you are checking your NHT filing status, confirm that your NIS Reconciliation for 2025 is also complete. This is the third of the three year-end submissions:

1. SO1 Annual Return → TAJ (eTAJ portal)
2. NHT Annual Return → NHT (NHT employer portal)
3. NIS Reconciliation → National Insurance Scheme (Ministry of Labour and Social Security)

The NIS Reconciliation confirms that your NIS contributions for every employee during 2025 are correctly recorded. NIS has its own contribution ceiling, its own rates (3% employee / 3% employer on insurable earnings), and its own reconciliation process.

Three submissions. Three portals. Three confirmation receipts. All three due by March 31.

If you are not certain all three are complete, open each portal right now and check. You have three days. Do not assume the SO1 covers it all — it does not.
