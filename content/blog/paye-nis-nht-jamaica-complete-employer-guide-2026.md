# PAYE, NIS, and NHT Jamaica: The Complete Employer Guide 2026

Every Jamaican employer has four statutory payroll obligations. Getting them right is not optional — it's legally mandated, and the penalties for errors are significant.

This guide covers all four: **PAYE** (Pay As You Earn income tax), **NIS** (National Insurance Scheme), **NHT** (National Housing Trust), and **Education Tax**. We explain how each works, the current 2026 rates, how to calculate correctly, the deadlines you cannot miss, and the most expensive mistakes Jamaican employers make.

## What Is the PAYE Threshold in Jamaica for 2026?

The PAYE income tax threshold in Jamaica for 2026 is J$1,500,096 per year (J$125,008 per month). Employees earning below this threshold pay no income tax under Jamaica's PAYE system.

## The Four Statutory Deductions: Quick Overview

| Deduction | Purpose | Employee Rate | Employer Rate | Who Receives |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| PAYE | Income tax withholding | 25% or 30% (on taxable income) | — | Tax Administration Jamaica (TAJ) |
| NHT | National Housing Trust fund | 2% | 3% | National Housing Trust |
| NIS | National Insurance (unemployment, disability, health) | 3% | 3% | National Insurance Scheme |
| Education Tax | Education fund | 2.25% | 3.5% | Tax Administration Jamaica (TAJ) |

All four must be calculated for each pay period, deducted from employees (employee portions), added on top by the employer (employer portions), and remitted by the **14th of the following month**.

## PAYE (Pay As You Earn) — Detailed Guide

### What Is PAYE?
PAYE is the mechanism by which employers withhold income tax from employee salaries and remit it to the Tax Administration Jamaica. The employer acts as a tax collector for the government — you hold the money and pay it to the TAJ each month.

**If you fail to withhold PAYE**, you — the employer — are liable for the tax, not the employee. This is one of the most serious payroll errors a Jamaican employer can make.

### 2026 PAYE Rates and Thresholds

| Annual Income | Tax Rate |
|---|---|
| Up to J$1,500,096 | 0% (exempt) |
| J$1,500,097 – J$6,000,000 | 25% |
| Above J$6,000,000 | 30% |

**Monthly equivalents:**
- Tax-free threshold: J$125,008/month
- 25% bracket: J$125,009 – J$500,000/month
- 30% bracket: Above J$500,000/month

**Special thresholds:**
- Employees aged 65+: Higher tax-free threshold applies — verify current figure with TAJ
- Employees with dependent relief or approved pension deductions: Adjustments may apply

### How PAYE Works Across the Year

PAYE is calculated on a **cumulative basis** across Jamaica's April 1 – March 31 tax year. This means:

- You don't just calculate PAYE on this month's salary in isolation
- You track the employee's **year-to-date gross income** and **year-to-date PAYE paid**
- Each month, you recalculate the year-to-date PAYE liability and withhold only what hasn't been paid yet

**Why this matters:** An employee who earns a large bonus in December could owe more PAYE than usual because their annual income has jumped into a higher bracket. A good payroll system catches this automatically. Manual calculations often miss it.

### Common PAYE Mistakes

1. **Using an outdated tax threshold** — Jamaica updates this periodically; the 2026 figure is J$1,500,096/year. Prior year figures will cause under-withholding.

2. **Not tracking cumulative income** — calculating PAYE month by month in isolation instead of cumulatively will produce the wrong result as soon as an employee's income profile changes.

3. **Forgetting PAYE applies to benefits-in-kind** — a company car used for personal travel, rent paid by the employer, and certain allowances above standard amounts are taxable benefits.

4. **Not filing even when no PAYE is owed** — employers must still file S01 returns for months with zero PAYE if they have employees. "No payment, no filing" is incorrect and leads to compliance gaps.

## NHT (National Housing Trust) — Detailed Guide

### What Is the NHT?
The National Housing Trust is a government-mandated savings and benefit scheme. Employee contributions accumulate toward their NHT balance, which they can use as a deposit or mortgage benefit when purchasing property.

**This is not a tax** — it's a benefit fund. Employees who leave employment can apply for a refund of their NHT contributions after a period. This makes it even more important to get NHT numbers and contributions right.

### 2026 NHT Rates

| Party | Rate | Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Employee | 2% | Gross wages (no cap) |
| Employer | 3% | Gross wages (no cap) |

**There is no income cap on NHT.** Unlike NIS, NHT contributions continue regardless of how much an employee earns. A J$1,000,000/month salary still attracts J$20,000 employee NHT and J$30,000 employer NHT.

### The NHT Number Is Critical

Every NHT contribution must be tied to the specific employee's NHT number. This is how the NHT tracks each employee's accumulated balance.

**If you remit NHT contributions without NHT numbers** (or with wrong numbers), those contributions are not credited to the correct employee. The employee's NHT balance shows zero despite you having paid, and they cannot access their housing benefit.

**Action:** Collect every employee's NHT number before running their first payroll. If an employee doesn't have one, assist them in registering at nhts.gov.jm.

### NHT Late Payment Penalties

NHT imposes a **20% per annum penalty** on outstanding contributions. This is aggressive — approximately 1.67% per month on overdue amounts.

For an employer with J$200,000/month in NHT contributions, a 3-month delay costs J$10,000 in penalties. A full year of late payments costs J$40,000. The math makes on-time payment non-optional.

## NIS (National Insurance Scheme) — Detailed Guide

### What Is the NIS?
The National Insurance Scheme provides benefits including retirement pension, employment injury benefits, maternity allowance, and funeral grants. Unlike NHT, NIS covers multiple life events, not just housing.

### 2026 NIS Rates

| Party | Rate | Maximum Insurable Wage |
|---|---|---|
| Employee | 3% | J$125,000/month (J$1,500,000/year) |
| Employer | 3% | Same cap |

### Understanding the NIS Wage Cap

NIS contributions are calculated on wages up to J$125,000/month. Once an employee's cumulative gross wages for the tax year reach J$1,500,000, NIS contributions stop for the remainder of the year and resume April 1.

**Example:**
- Employee earns J$180,000/month
- NIS is calculated on J$125,000, not J$180,000
- Monthly employee NIS: J$125,000 × 3% = **J$3,750**
- Monthly employer NIS: J$125,000 × 3% = **J$3,750**
- The J$55,000 above the cap is not subject to NIS

**Annual cap hit timing:** At J$180,000/month, this employee reaches the J$1,500,000 annual NIS cap in month 8.3 of the tax year (around mid-November). From that point, no NIS is deducted or contributed for the remainder of the year.

### Common NIS Mistakes

1. **Continuing NIS deductions past the annual cap** — overpaying NIS contributions is an employer error that requires a correction process.

2. **Not tracking per-employee cumulative wages** — the cap applies per employee, not per payroll run. You need running totals per person.

3. **Missing NIS registration for new employees** — employees must be registered with NIS before their first payroll. If they don't have an NIS number, obtain one before processing.

## Education Tax — The Most Overlooked Deduction

### What Is Education Tax?
Education Tax is an employer and employee levy that funds Jamaica's education system. It's frequently missed because it doesn't have a separate registration, separate return, or separate payment — it's reported and remitted on the same S01 return as PAYE.

Many employers who audit their historic payroll find they've been paying PAYE, NHT, and NIS correctly but omitting Education Tax entirely.

### 2026 Education Tax Rates

| Party | Rate | Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Employee | 2.25% | Gross wages |
| Employer | 3.5% | Gross wages |

**No cap.** Education Tax applies to the full gross wage, same as NHT.

## Monthly Filing Deadlines: The 14th Rule

All four statutory payments are due by the **14th of the following month**:

| Month | Payment Due Date |
|---|---|
| January | February 14 |
| February | March 14 |
| March | April 14 |
| ... | ... |

This applies to:
- PAYE remittance to TAJ
- NHT contributions
- NIS contributions
- Education Tax remittance to TAJ

If the 14th falls on a weekend or public holiday, contact each agency for guidance — they sometimes extend but do not always.

**Best practice:** Complete payroll and initiate all payments by the 10th of the month. This gives you a 4-day buffer for banking processing and any corrections.

## Total Employer Payroll Cost — Full Picture

A common mistake is treating an employee's salary as the total cost of employment. The employer's statutory contributions add significantly to the real cost.

**Example: Employee earning J$150,000/month**

| Cost | Amount |
|---|---|
| Gross salary | J$150,000 |
| NHT (employer 3%) | J$4,500 |
| NIS (employer 3%, on J$125,000 cap) | J$3,750 |
| Education Tax (employer 3.5%) | J$5,250 |
| **Total monthly employment cost** | **J$163,500** |

The employer is paying **J$13,500/month** in statutory contributions on top of the J$150,000 salary. On an annualised basis, that's J$162,000 in employer contributions. Budget for this from day one.

## PAYE vs. NHT vs. NIS: Key Differences at a Glance

| Feature | PAYE | NHT | NIS |
|---|---|---|---|
| Purpose | Income tax | Housing savings | Social insurance |
| Employee only or both? | Employee only | Both | Both |
| Income cap? | No cap (rates change by bracket) | No cap | J$1,500,000/year |
| Who receives payment? | TAJ | NHT | NIS |
| Employee gets benefit? | No (it's a tax) | Yes (housing deposits) | Yes (pensions, maternity, injury) |
| Late payment penalty | TAJ interest + penalties | 20% per annum | Penalties apply |
