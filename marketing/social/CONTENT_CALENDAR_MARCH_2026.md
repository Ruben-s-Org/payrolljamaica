*Theme: NIS — What Most Employers Get Wrong*

**Copy:**
NIS is the "quiet one" of Jamaica's statutory deductions — and that's exactly why it creates problems.

Three things Jamaican employers often get wrong with NIS:

**1. The insurable wage ceiling**
NIS contributions are capped at the insurable wage ceiling set by the Ministry of Labour. In 2026, if you haven't checked the updated ceiling recently — check it today. Overpaying costs you cash. Underpaying triggers penalties.

**2. The employer-employee split**
Both employer and employee pay 3% NIS. This is symmetrical and non-negotiable. Some payroll processors only calculate the employee side.

**3. Reporting casual and contract workers**
If a contractor works regularly for you, NIS may still apply depending on the nature of the engagement. "They invoice me" doesn't automatically mean "NIS exempt."

NIS funds employee sick leave, maternity benefits, and retirement — getting it right protects your team as much as your compliance record.

**Copy:**
TAJ's online portal (TAJ Online at toj.taj.gov.jm) is how most Jamaican employers file and pay PAYE, NHT, NIS, and Education Tax.

But using it doesn't mean you're compliant. Here's what employers miss:

**Filing vs. Payment are two separate actions**
Many employers file their return on time but delay the payment. TAJ treats these independently — a filed return with late payment still attracts penalties.

**The system doesn't validate your math**
TAJ Online accepts what you submit. If your calculation is wrong, it takes the wrong number. The portal is a submission tool, not a compliance checker.

**Multiple employer registration numbers create confusion**
If your business has multiple branches or entities with different employer numbers, each one needs its own reconciliation. They don't merge automatically.

### WEDNESDAY, MARCH 25

**LINKEDIN**
*Theme: Education Tax — The One Everyone Forgets*

**Copy:**
Nobody talks about Education Tax. And that's exactly why it's a year-end compliance problem.

In Jamaica, Education Tax is deducted separately from PAYE and calculated on statutory income (not gross income — there's a difference).

**2026 Education Tax rates:**
- Employer: 3.5% on statutory income
- Employee: 2.25% on statutory income

**What's statutory income?**
Statutory income is gross emoluments minus NIS contributions. So the calculation order matters:
1. Calculate NIS first
2. Subtract NIS from gross emoluments
3. Apply Education Tax to the result
4. THEN apply PAYE to the remaining taxable income

Getting the calculation order wrong means Education Tax and PAYE are both slightly off. Multiply that small error across 12 months and 30 employees and you have a real gap.
