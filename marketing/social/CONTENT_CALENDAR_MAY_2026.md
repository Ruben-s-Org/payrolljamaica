*Theme: NHT Refunds — The Employee Benefit Nobody Talks About*

```
Most Jamaican employees contributing to NHT don't know they can get their money back.

After 7 years of continuous contributions, an NHT contributor is entitled to a refund of their employee contributions. It's not widely publicised, but it's real, it's significant, and it's money your employees are entitled to.

Here's what employers should know:

The NHT refund is processed by the National Housing Trust and typically takes 4–6 months once applied for. Employees apply directly to NHT — they don't need employer involvement for the basic refund. However, employers can help by:

1. Informing employees that this benefit exists
2. Ensuring contribution records are accurate and up to date (refund applications require proof of all contributions)
3. Providing correct NHT contribution history in employee records

If your payroll records are clean and accessible, your employees can apply and receive their refund with minimal friction. If records are patchy — applications stall.

Tell your team. It's their money.
```

### WEDNESDAY, MAY 13

**LINKEDIN**
*Theme: Education Tax — Still the Most Misunderstood Deduction*

```
Quick question: what exactly does Education Tax fund?

If you're deducting it from every payslip but can't answer that — you're not alone.

Education Tax funds Jamaica's education system through the Education Tax Act. It's not optional, and it's not discretionary. Here's the structure:

Employee pays: 2.25% of statutory income
Employer pays: 3.5% of statutory income (yes, you pay more than they do)

Statutory income = gross income MINUS NIS contributions. So NIS is calculated first, then Education Tax is applied to the result.

That ordering matters. Get the sequence wrong and both your PAYE and your Education Tax calculations are off.

The most common error: employers calculate Education Tax on gross salary, skipping the NIS deduction step. It's a small percentage difference per employee per month — but across a full year, across a full team, it adds up.

PayrollJamaica calculates Education Tax in the correct sequence automatically. It also generates the correct employer-side Education Tax liability for your records.
```

### MONDAY, MAY 18

**LINKEDIN**
*Theme: How to Onboard a New Employee Correctly in Jamaica*

```
Hiring someone new? Here's what Jamaica labour law requires before their first payroll run.

Before an employee's first payday, you need:

1. TRN (Taxpayer Registration Number)
Without their TRN, you cannot correctly calculate PAYE. Ensure they have one before day one.

2. NIS Number
Required for NIS contribution registration. If they don't have one, they need to register at NIS.

3. NHT Number
Required for NHT contribution registration. Again — get this on day one or you're retroactively fixing contribution records.

4. Written employment contract
Under the Employment (Equal Opportunity and Treatment) Act, employees must receive a written contract. It must cover: salary, hours, leave entitlements, termination notice periods.

5. New employee declaration form
File with TAJ for income tax purposes.

6. Add to payroll records
Name, TRN, NIS number, NHT number, date of commencement, agreed salary, employment type (full-time/part-time/contract).

Miss any of these and your first payroll for this employee is non-compliant.
```

```
Whit Monday is May 25 — a Jamaica public holiday.

Here's what every employer needs to know about public holiday pay:

Under Jamaica's Holidays with Pay Act:

1. If an employee works on a public holiday, they must be paid at LEAST double their regular rate (2x).

2. If an employee does NOT work on a public holiday, they are still entitled to a full day's pay — provided they are a regular employee who has worked the preceding workday.

3. Overtime on public holidays: if they work more than their regular hours, additional overtime rules may apply.

Many Jamaican businesses handle this incorrectly — either not paying the premium or incorrectly calculating the base rate for the double-time calculation.
```

*Theme: When Should a Jamaican Business Hire Its First HR Person?*

```
The question I hear from Jamaica business owners as they grow:

"When do I need an HR person?"

Here's a practical framework:

1–5 employees: The founder handles HR. Payroll can be managed manually or with software.

5–15 employees: HR starts taking 3–5 hours a week. This is where most owners feel the pain — payroll errors, compliance questions, employee issues. A payroll tool is essential here. A part-time HR consultant is worth considering.

15–30 employees: A dedicated HR function is necessary. At this size, payroll complexity, employment contracts, performance management, and compliance all require dedicated attention. A full-time HR Manager or a fractional HR Director is the norm.

30+ employees: Full HR team. A dedicated payroll specialist. Compliance frameworks. Employee handbook. Onboarding process.

The mistake: waiting until 30 employees to build what you needed at 15. When you hire your first HR person, they spend the first 6 months fixing the errors from the years before they arrived.

The solution: start with good systems early. A proper payroll platform at employee #2 means your first HR hire inherits clean records.
```
