**LinkedIn Post 2 — April 8 (Education Week)**

New employees hired in April are the most expensive ones to get wrong.

Here's why: When you onboard a new employee mid-year, you inherit their year-to-date tax position from their previous employer. Get the carryforward wrong, and you're either under-deducting PAYE (they'll owe at year-end) or over-deducting (they'll resent you for eating their take-home pay).

The right process for onboarding a new employee in April in Jamaica:

1. Collect their TD4 or tax coding notice from TAJ
2. Confirm their NIS registration — new employees without NIS numbers need to register before their first payroll run
3. Get their NHT beneficiary number — separate from TRN and NIS
4. Enter the TD4 figures into your payroll system BEFORE running their first paycheck

This is the difference between a compliant onboarding and a compliance problem that surfaces in March 2027.

PayrollJamaica's onboarding workflow guides you through each step automatically — including the TD4 carryforward calculation.

**Twitter Thread 2 — April 15 (Automation Feature Week)**

THREAD: What "automated payroll" actually means for a Jamaican business. Because it's not just about saving time. 🧵

1/ "Automated payroll" is a phrase that gets thrown around. Let's be specific about what it means — and what it saves you from — in a Jamaican compliance context.

2/ Every month, a Jamaican employer needs to:
- Calculate PAYE for every employee (on their taxable income, applying the correct threshold)
- Calculate NIS at 3% employee / 3% employer (on insurable earnings, not gross)
- Calculate NHT at 2% employee / 3% employer (on gross)
- Calculate Education Tax at 2.25% employee / 3.5% employer
- Calculate HEART at 3% employer only
That's 5 separate calculations, per employee, per month.

3/ Manual: you do this in a spreadsheet, applying rates you looked up (and hope haven't changed), for every employee. Then you prepare bank transfer instructions. Then you prepare the TAJ remittance schedule. Then the NHT schedule. Then NIS.

4/ Automated: you enter hours worked (or it's already there from a timekeeping integration). You click "run payroll." Every calculation is done, every statutory deduction is correct, every file is generated.

5/ The compliance value isn't just time. It's that the software tracks legislative changes. When NIS updates its insurable earnings ceiling, it updates automatically. You don't have to remember to check. You don't have to update a spreadsheet formula.

6/ For a business with 10 employees, manual payroll takes 4–6 hours per cycle. Automated payroll takes 20–30 minutes. That's 3–5 hours back per month. Over a year: 36–60 hours.
