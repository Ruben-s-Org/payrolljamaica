Let me explain NHT contributions in a way that makes both employers AND employees care about getting them right.

Every month, your employees contribute 2% of their gross wages to NHT. You add 3% on top. That money doesn't disappear — it builds toward two very real benefits.

**Benefit 1: The NHT Mortgage**

NHT offers home loans at rates as low as 0% to qualifying contributors. Compare that to commercial bank mortgage rates in Jamaica: typically 8–10%. Over a 30-year mortgage on a $12M property:

→ At 0% NHT rate: repayments based purely on principal
→ At 9% commercial rate: total interest paid could exceed $30M over the loan life

This is a life-changing difference. And your employees are building toward it with every payslip.

**Benefit 2: The 7-Year Refund**

After 7 years of contributions, employees who haven't accessed the housing benefit are eligible for a full refund of their employee contributions, with interest. Many Jamaicans don't know this. Share this post with your team.

When an employee applies for their NHT mortgage or refund, NHT cross-checks their contribution history against employer remittance records. If your records show deductions but your remittances don't match — the employee's application stalls.

Some employers deduct NHT from employee wages but don't remit to NHT. That's a serious legal violation. But even unintentional gaps — late filings, incorrect wage bases — can cause problems for employees at the worst possible time.

Five days away. This post covers the full legal picture. Read it. Share it with your operations team. Save it for your records.

---

**WHICH LAW APPLIES?**

The Holidays (Public General) Act. This is the legislation that governs how all gazetted public holidays are handled in Jamaica — including pay entitlements for both working and non-working employees.

---

**SCENARIO 1: Your employee does NOT work on Labour Day**

Entitlement: One full day's pay at their regular daily rate.

Condition: They must have worked the last scheduled working day before the holiday (Friday, May 22 for most businesses). If an employee was absent without authorisation on May 22, the employer can legally deny the holiday pay. This must be documented with an attendance record.

---

**SCENARIO 2: Your employee DOES work on Labour Day**

Entitlement: TWO options, employer's choice (though best practice is to discuss with employee):

→ **Option A:** Double time — 2x their regular daily rate for hours worked. If their daily rate is $4,000 JMD, they are paid $8,000 for Labour Day.

→ **Option B:** Regular pay for the day + a full compensatory day off in lieu at a mutually agreed time.

Note: If the employee works more than their regular hours on Labour Day, overtime rules stack on top of the public holiday premium.

---

**WHO DOES THIS AFFECT MOST?**

Any employer with staff working May 23:
→ Hotels and resorts
→ Restaurants and food service
→ Retail stores open on the holiday
→ Security companies
→ Hospitals and private healthcare
→ Transportation and logistics

---

**THE MISTAKE TO AVOID:**

Paying Labour Day at regular rates. This is a wage violation. Ministry of Labour claims for underpaid public holiday pay are common and time-consuming to resolve. The underpayment plus interest and any dispute costs will always exceed the original correct payment.

---

**HOW TO SET THIS UP IN YOUR PAYROLL:**

If you're using PayrollJamaica: Labour Day is already on the Jamaica gazetted holiday calendar. Flag the employees working on May 23. The system applies the correct rate automatically.

If you're running manual payroll: Identify employees rostered for May 23. Calculate 2x daily rate for those working. Document the calculation. Generate pay slips that show the Labour Day premium separately from regular wages.

Happy post-Labour Day Monday, Jamaica. 🇯🇲 Hope the weekend treated you well.

This week's deep-dive is for every hotel, resort, and restaurant operator in Jamaica.

**The service charge payroll compliance problem — let's talk about it.**

The Tourism Service Charge Act requires that the service charge collected from guests be distributed to qualifying employees. Most Jamaica hospitality operators know this.

What many don't know is that the distribution must be:

**1. Run through the payroll system — not as a cash payment outside payroll.**
Service charge is earnings. It creates PAYE liability. Employers who distribute it as cash without running it through payroll are creating an underreported income situation for TAJ. If audited, this generates back-tax assessments for every employee who received the cash distribution, plus employer penalty exposure.

**2. Included in statutory deduction calculations.**
When calculating PAYE, NHT, NIS, and Education Tax — the service charge distribution is part of the employee's total employment income. You can't calculate NHT on base salary alone and exclude service charge.

**3. Separately itemized on pay slips.**
Employees should see: Base salary + Service charge distribution = Total gross wages. Then deductions applied to the total. This is the only way to generate the documentation TAJ requires.

**What the correct process looks like:**
→ Calculate total service charge collected for the period
→ Apply the allocation formula per the Act (typically split between eligible staff by hours/grade)
→ Run as a separate pay element in your payroll system
→ Generate pay slips showing service charge as a distinct line item
→ All statutory deductions calculated on total income (base + service charge)
→ PAYE and NHT remittances based on total income
