### SCRIPT 1 — PAYE Basics
**Trigger question:** "Can someone explain PAYE in Jamaica? I just hired my first employee."

**Answer:**
> Congrats on the hire! Here's PAYE simplified:
>
> PAYE (Pay As You Earn) is income tax you deduct from your employee's salary every pay period and remit to TAJ by the 14th of the following month.
>
> Key numbers for 2025–26 tax year:
> - Tax-free threshold: J$1,500,096/year (J$125,008/month)
> - Above threshold: 25% up to J$6M annual income, then 30%
>
> Practical example: Employee earning J$200,000/month
> - Monthly threshold: J$125,008
> - Taxable: J$74,992
> - PAYE to deduct: ~J$18,748 (25%)
>
> You also pay NHT (3%), NIS (3%), and Education Tax (3.5%) as the employer on top of the salary.
>
> Miss the 14th deadline? 50% penalty on the outstanding PAYE. TAJ enforces this.
>
> Happy to answer follow-up questions — we cover Jamaica payroll compliance weekly at PayrollJamaica.com if you ever want a free calculator to check the numbers.

### SCRIPT 2 — NHT Confusion
**Trigger question:** "Does NHT apply to part-time workers?" or "I'm confused about who pays NHT"

**Answer:**
> Good question — NHT trips up a lot of business owners.
>
> Here's how it works:
>
> **Who pays NHT:**
> - Employees working 8+ hours per week → both employer AND employee contribute
> - Employees working less than 8 hours/week → generally exempt
>
> **Rates:**
> - Employer pays: 3% of gross emoluments
> - Employee pays: 2% of gross emoluments
>
> **The common mistake:** Only calculating on base salary. NHT should be on "gross emoluments" — which includes housing allowances, car allowances, and other regular payments. If you're only running it on base salary, you may be underpaying.
>
> **Late penalty:** 20% on overdue contributions. Not 20% annually — 20% flat. On a J$100K contribution that's J$20K in penalties for being late.
>
> If you want to double-check your numbers, PayrollJamaica has a free calculator that handles NHT, NIS, PAYE and Education Tax simultaneously → payrolljamaica.com/calculator

### SCRIPT 3 — March 31 Deadline (High urgency — use in Week 2)
**Trigger question:** "What exactly do I need to have done by March 31?" or any year-end anxiety post

**Answer:**
> March 31 is the end of Jamaica's fiscal year. Here's what needs to be sorted before or on that date:
>
> **What TAJ expects by March 31:**
> 1. All 12 monthly PAYE remittances filed (S01 forms) — one per month
> 2. All NHT contributions reconciled and remitted monthly
> 3. All NIS contributions up to date
> 4. Education Tax calculated and remitted
>
> **What comes AFTER March 31 (by April 14):**
> - Annual employer return (P6 form for each employee)
> - Annual payroll reconciliation report
>
> So the hard deadline for monthly remittances is March 14 (for February payroll). If you have any outstanding months — now is the time to file.
>
> If you're not sure where you stand, run a free reconciliation check at payrolljamaica.com/calculator — it catches discrepancies across all 4 deduction types in under 5 minutes.

### SCRIPT 4 — True Cost of an Employee
**Trigger question:** "How much does it really cost to hire someone?" or salary negotiation questions

**Answer:**
> This catches a lot of business owners by surprise.
>
> Agreed salary ≠ total cost to you as employer.
>
> For a J$100,000/month employee, your actual cost is:
>
> | Item | Amount |
> |------|--------|
> | Gross salary | J$100,000 |
> | NHT (employer, 3%) | J$3,000 |
> | NIS (employer, 3%) | J$3,000 |
> | Education Tax (employer, 3.5%) | J$3,500 |
> | **Total employer cost** | **J$109,500** |
>
> That's nearly 10% on top. If you're hiring 5 people at J$100K, you're spending J$548,750/month — not J$500,000.
>
> This matters most when you're deciding whether you can afford to hire. Always budget the full employer cost, not just the salary.
>
> (PayrollJamaica's calculator shows you the full employer cost before you commit — free at payrolljamaica.com/calculator)

### SCRIPT 5 — Excel vs Payroll Software
**Trigger question:** "Is it okay to just use Excel for payroll?" or "Do I really need payroll software?"

**Answer:**
> Excel works — until it doesn't.
>
> Most Jamaican SMEs start on spreadsheets. The problems show up when:
> - PAYE thresholds change (they do every April) and you forget to update formulas
> - Someone enters the wrong NIS rate and it compounds 12 months before you catch it
> - You're prepping for a TAJ audit and can't produce clean, verifiable records
> - You have more than 5 employees and one wrong formula affects everyone
>
> The real question is: what's the cost of a J$50,000+ penalty vs what payroll software costs?
>
> That said — if you're going to use Excel, at minimum:
> - Update your PAYE thresholds every April 1 (new tax year)
> - Use the current NHT/NIS/Education Tax rates (they change)
> - Keep a separate running total for each deduction type per employee
> - Reconcile monthly before you remit to TAJ
>
> If you want to check if your current numbers are correct, the free calculator at payrolljamaica.com/calculator runs all four deductions at once and flags errors.

### SCRIPT 6 — Employee vs Contractor (For HR Jamaica Network / Accountants)
**Trigger question:** "What's the difference between an employee and a contractor for tax purposes in Jamaica?"

**Answer:**
> This is one of the most common TAJ audit triggers — misclassifying employees as contractors.
>
> **Contractor (no statutory deductions required):**
> - Works for multiple clients
> - Controls when/how the work is done
> - Provides own tools/equipment
> - No guaranteed work continuity
> - Should have a clear service agreement
>
> **Employee (full statutory deductions required):**
> - Works set hours for one employer
> - Employer controls how work is done
> - Uses employer's tools
> - Ongoing, regular work relationship
>
> TAJ looks at the substance of the relationship, not just what you call it. If someone looks and acts like an employee, TAJ will treat them as one — and back-charge all PAYE, NHT, NIS and Education Tax you should have deducted, plus penalties.
>
> The safe rule: if in doubt, treat as an employee.
>
> If you've recently reclassified someone or are unsure about existing arrangements, it's worth a conversation with a tax advisor before March 31.
