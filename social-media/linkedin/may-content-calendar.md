Two months into Jamaica's new tax year. This week, before you process your next payroll, take one hour to do a Q1 reconciliation. It might be the most valuable hour you spend on compliance all year.

**What is a Q1 Jamaica payroll reconciliation?**

For the purposes of the new tax year (which started April 1), Q1 here means the quarter January–March 2026 — the last quarter of the prior tax year. It's the period your S01 Annual Return will reflect when it's due next March. Errors from that period compound. Catching them now, while records are fresh, prevents TAJ queries later.

**The Q1 Reconciliation — 5 Questions:**

**1. Did your PAYE calculations match your remittances?**
Pull your January, February, and March S01 filings. Compare the PAYE declared against the payments made. Any gap is a liability — either an underpayment (interest accruing) or an overpayment (credits you can claim).

**2. Did your NIS contributions reconcile with the NIS schedule?**
Cross-reference what your payroll records show you remitted to the National Insurance Scheme against what NIS has on file. Discrepancies are common in manual payroll environments. They need to be corrected formally.

**3. Were all NHT employer contributions remitted on time?**
NHT is due the 14th of the following month. For Jan–Mar payroll, those deadlines were Feb 14, March 14, April 14. Confirm each was filed and paid. NHT tracks employer remittances against employee benefit eligibility — late remittances affect your employees, not just your compliance record.

**4. Did every employee receive pay slips for each pay period?**
Pay slips are documentation. In any employment dispute, pay slips for the relevant period are the first evidence requested. If January or February pay slips were not issued or are not on record, generate them now.

**5. Are there any employees whose TRN was missing for any January–March payroll?**
TAJ S01 filings require TRNs. Filings without a valid TRN are flagged. If any employee started in Q1 without a TRN being collected immediately, those months may need amended filings.

### POST 7 — Monday, May 18, 2026
**Theme:** Labour Day Pay Rules — Authoritative Guide
**Format:** Detailed educational post
**Best time to post:** 8:00 AM Jamaica time

**CAPTION:**

Five days until Labour Day (May 23). This is the post every Jamaica employer running operations over the holiday needs to read and share with their payroll team.

**Jamaica Labour Day Pay — The Legal Framework:**

Under the **Holidays (Public General) Act**, Labour Day is a gazetted public holiday in Jamaica. This means:

**For employees who DO NOT work:**
They are entitled to a full day's pay at their regular daily rate, provided they worked the day before the public holiday (or the last scheduled working day before it). This is not a discretionary benefit — it is a statutory right.

**For employees who DO work:**
They are entitled to:
→ Option A: Double their regular daily rate for hours worked on Labour Day (2x pay for the day), OR
→ Option B: Their regular pay for the holiday hours PLUS a full day off in lieu at a time agreed between employer and employee

**The "day before" rule:**
An employee who is absent without authorization on the working day before Labour Day loses their entitlement to public holiday pay. This is a legitimate basis for denying the holiday pay — but it must be documented with an attendance record. "I think they were absent" is not documentation.

**Sectors most affected:**
→ Hotels and resorts (full operations on Labour Day)
→ Restaurants and food service
→ Retail (especially large-format retail open on the holiday)
→ Security firms
→ Healthcare and private hospitals
→ Transportation and logistics

**The payroll calculation mistake employers make:**
Running Labour Day hours at the regular rate and hoping no one notices. In Jamaica's current labour environment, employees are increasingly aware of their statutory rights. Ministry of Labour claims for unpaid public holiday pay are common, take time to resolve, and cost more than the original underpayment.

### POST 10 — Monday, May 25, 2026
**Theme:** Service Charge Compliance — Hospitality Sector
**Format:** Niche educational deep-dive
**Best time to post:** 8:30 AM Jamaica time

**CAPTION:**

For Jamaica's hospitality and food service employers — this one is specifically for you.

The service charge is one of the most frequently mishandled payroll elements in Jamaica's tourism sector. Getting it wrong is a compliance risk with TAJ, the Tourism Product Development Company, and the Ministry of Labour simultaneously.

**What the Jamaica Tourism Service Charge Act requires:**

Hotels, restaurants, and tourism service providers who charge a service fee (typically 10–15% on bills) are required to distribute that service charge to qualifying employees. The distribution must happen within statutory timeframes — and the amounts distributed must be:
→ Treated as part of the employee's wages for PAYE purposes
→ Reported on pay slips separately from base salary
→ Included in the calculation of statutory deductions (NHT, NIS, Education Tax applied to total employment income)
→ Tracked and documented for audit purposes

**The common mistakes:**
1. Treating service charge as a cash distribution outside the payroll system — it still generates PAYE liability
2. Applying statutory deductions to base salary only, excluding service charge income
3. Not issuing pay slips that separately itemize service charge amounts
4. Distributing service charge in ways that don't meet the Act's allocation formula

**If TAJ audits a hotel or restaurant and finds service charge income was not run through payroll:** The employer faces back PAYE liability for every affected employee, plus interest and potentially penalties. In a hotel with 80 staff receiving regular service charge, this can be a material amount.

PayrollJamaica supports service charge pay runs as a separate payroll element — taxed correctly, reported separately on pay slips, included in all statutory calculations, and documented for audit readiness.
