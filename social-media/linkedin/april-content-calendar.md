### POST 5 — Friday, April 11, 2026
**Theme:** Common Payroll Mistakes — Educational
**Format:** List post
**Best time to post:** 11:00 AM Jamaica time

**CAPTION:**

I've reviewed hundreds of Jamaica payroll runs. These are the 5 most expensive mistakes I keep seeing — and they're all avoidable.

**Mistake #1: Using last year's tax threshold**
The income tax threshold in Jamaica is not static. It changes. When a new tax year starts on April 1, some employers forget to update their PAYE calculations. The result: employees are overtaxed, they complain, HR spends hours correcting it, and TAJ may still penalize you for incorrect remittances.

**Mistake #2: Wrong NIS insurable wage ceiling**
NIS has a maximum insurable wage — contributions are capped at that ceiling. Employers who don't update this ceiling overpay employer NIS contributions all year. Across 20 employees, that's a material overpayment.

**Mistake #3: Not remitting on time to TAJ**
Tax Administration Jamaica has strict deadlines. Employer PAYE remittances are due by the 14th of the following month. Late payments attract interest and penalties. This is avoidable — set up automated remittance reminders or use software that tracks your due dates.

**Mistake #4: Missing the statutory income deduction**
Under Jamaica's PAYE rules, employees are entitled to a deduction of 1/3 of their gross income (up to $500,000) before tax is calculated. Employers who miss this are over-deducting PAYE, leaving employees with less take-home than they're legally entitled to. Your best employees will leave for employers who calculate this correctly.

**Mistake #5: Manual payroll with no audit trail**
When TAJ audits your payroll records (and they do audit), you need to produce clear documentation of every calculation, every deduction, every remittance. A spreadsheet with no version history is a compliance risk.

April and May are peak hiring months for Jamaica businesses. Retailers hiring for summer, hotels ramping up for tourist season, construction and infrastructure projects starting their Q2 cycle.

If you're bringing on new employees this month, here's what the payroll onboarding looks like in Jamaica — and where employers commonly go wrong.

**Step 1: Collect the TRN**
Every employee must have a Tax Registration Number (TRN) from Tax Administration Jamaica. You cannot correctly calculate PAYE without a valid TRN. If your new hire doesn't have one, they need to register at TAJ before their first pay period. Without this, you're running non-compliant payroll.

**Step 2: Register with NIS**
New employees must be registered with the National Insurance Scheme. If they've never been formally employed before, this is their first NIS number. Process this before the first payroll run.

**Step 3: Register with NHT**
Same principle. NHT requires employer registration for each new employee. Do this early — NHT contributions must begin from the first pay period.

**Step 4: Set Up the Employment Contract**
Jamaica's Labour Relations and Industrial Disputes Act (LRIDA) and the Employment (Termination and Redundancy Payments) Act provide the framework. Ensure your contracts are compliant, especially for probationary periods and termination clauses.

**2. Did your NIS contributions reconcile against the NIS schedule?**
Run a comparison between what your payroll system says you remitted to NIS versus what NIS has on record. Discrepancies are common when payroll is manual and need to be corrected before they compound.

Pay slips are not just a courtesy in Jamaica — they are documentation that employees are entitled to. If there's ever a labour dispute, clear pay slips are your first line of defence.

A common compliance gap: employees start in January, TRN collected in March, three months of PAYE filed without a TRN. TAJ can flag this.

Your payroll journal entries should reconcile with your balance sheet. If your accountant hasn't already done this reconciliation, now is the time.
